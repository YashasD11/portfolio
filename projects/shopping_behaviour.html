Customer Shopping Behaviour Analysis and Predictive Modeling


1. The Data-Driven Retail Environment

In the highly competitive retail sector, historical sales data is the key to anticipated future trends and optimizing inventory management. This comprehensive analytical project dives deep into a vast dataset of transactional retail history, aiming to dissect intricate customer shopping habits. The primary objective is to transition from simply reporting past sales to aggressively forecasting future purchasing behaviors, thereby allowing retail stakeholders to deploy proactive, data-informed operational strategies rather than reactive guesswork.

2. Data Cleansing and Aggregation

The foundation of this analysis rested upon extensive data wrangling using the powerful Python Pandas library. The raw transactional logs contained inconsistencies, missing values, and varying date formats that required harmonization. I executed a thorough cleaning pipeline, standardizing datatypes and handling null values through contextual imputation. A critical component was temporal aggregation; I transformed granular, line-by-line receipt data into aggregated longitudinal features per unique customer ID. This process calculated vital Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) metrics, encompassing recency (time since last purchase), frequency (total number of transactions), and monetary value (average basket size).

3. Exploratory Data Analysis and Visualization

Before implementing complex algorithms, I generated intuitive visual dashboards using Matplotlib, Seaborn, and Power BI. This Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) phase was crucial for uncovering macro-level retail trends. I visualized daily and seasonal sales cycles, identified the highest revenue-generating product categories, and mapped geographic customer distributions. These compelling visualizations were instrumental in communicating initial, high-level business insights directly to hypothetical non-technical stakeholders, proving that effective data science is as much about communication as it is about mathematics.

4. Predictive Modeling for Churn and Recommendations

Moving beyond descriptive analytics, I implemented predictive machine learning models to solve specific retail challenges. Firstly, I utilized a Random Forest Classifier to predict the probability of customer churn (attrition) based on their changing RFM (Recency, Frequency, Monetary) metrics, allowing for targeted re-engagement campaigns. Secondly, I explored collaborative filtering techniques to lay the groundwork for a preliminary product recommendation engine, suggesting items to users based on the purchasing history of mathematically similar consumer profiles. This dual approach emphasizes the tangible, revenue-generating impact of applied data science within the modern retail workflow.
